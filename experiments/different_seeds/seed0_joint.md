Despite the slower progress during later epochs, small improvements were still observed near the end of training, indicating that the model had not completely converged before the scheduled termination of the experiment.

The more challenging distributed configurations continued to benefit from joint training, achieving approximately 52.85% accuracy on `distribute_four`, 52.20% on `distribute_nine`, and 67.90% on `in_distri`. These values remained substantially higher than the corresponding single-task results, supporting the hypothesis that joint training facilitates knowledge transfer between different problem configurations.

The difference between the two runs remained below one percentage point, suggesting that the replicated model is relatively robust to random initialization. This close agreement indicates that the observed performance is not driven by a particularly favorable or unfavorable seed and that the remaining discrepancy with the results reported in the original publication is more likely attributable to systematic factors, such as dataset preprocessing, implementation details, or differences in the training procedure.

Overall, the first seed experiment provides evidence that the replicated preprocessed joint-training pipeline produces consistent results and that the improvements observed for the difficult configurations are reproducible.
