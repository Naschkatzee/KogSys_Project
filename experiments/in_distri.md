The in_distri experiment was conducted using the same technical setup as all previous experiments in order to maintain direct comparability across the entire replication suite. Training was performed for 300 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.005 and a weight decay of 0.01. The training batch size was set to 128, while evaluation used a batch size of 32. The experiment relied on the generated I-RAVEN dataset together with the prepared train, validation, and test index files. Logs, checkpoints, summaries, and metadata were continuously stored throughout execution.

The experiment completed successfully and remained technically stable during all 300 epochs. However, unlike the simpler configurations such as center_single, left_right, up_down, and in_out, the final performance was substantially lower. After 300 epochs, the model achieved approximately 85.8% training accuracy and approximately 61.4% test accuracy. Both training and test accuracies fluctuated moderately during the later epochs but generally remained within a relatively narrow range without sustained long-term improvement.

The results suggest limited but noticeable overfitting. The training accuracy consistently remained significantly higher than the test accuracy, with a persistent generalization gap of roughly 20–25 percentage points during the later training stages. Unlike the easier configurations, where train and test performance remained nearly identical, the in_distri configuration appears considerably more difficult for the reproduced SCL implementation. Nevertheless, the training accuracy itself did not converge toward 100%, indicating that the model was not merely memorizing the training set. Instead, the model appears to struggle with both optimization and generalization simultaneously on this task.

From a reasoning perspective, the in_distri configuration seems to belong to the same category of difficult relational tasks as distribute_four and distribute_nine. All three configurations involve more complex distributed object interactions and substantially larger relational search spaces than the simpler partition-based tasks. The observed performance pattern therefore strengthens the hypothesis emerging throughout the replication process: the reconstructed implementation successfully reproduces the original SCL behavior on simpler spatial reasoning configurations, while difficulties remain concentrated specifically in highly distributed multi-object relational settings.

At the same time, the experiment further validates the robustness of the reconstructed training pipeline itself. Training remained numerically stable throughout all epochs, losses stayed bounded, checkpointing functioned correctly, and the experiment completed without runtime failures. Consequently, the lower performance is unlikely to originate from instability in the execution environment. Instead, the discrepancy with the stronger results reported in the original paper most likely reflects remaining differences between the adapted implementation and the original framework, particularly because several unavailable jactorch components had to be manually replaced with simplified PyTorch implementations during the compatibility restoration process.

With the completion of in_distri, the full suite of single-task I-RAVEN configurations evaluated in the original paper has now been replicated. The results reveal a clear and consistent pattern across tasks: near-perfect reproduction was achieved for simpler spatial partition configurations, while more complex distributed multi-object reasoning tasks remain substantially harder for the reconstructed implementation.
