To evaluate the influence of random initialization on model performance, the best-performing preprocessed joint-training configuration was trained using five different random seeds. For each run, identical hyperparameters and dataset splits were used, while the Python, NumPy, and PyTorch random seeds were varied. The resulting validation accuracies exhibited remarkably low variability across runs.

The five seed experiments achieved best validation accuracies of 82.44%, 82.00%, 82.16%, 82.33%, and 82.25%, respectively. This corresponds to a mean validation accuracy of 82.24% with a standard deviation of approximately 0.17 percentage points. The difference between the strongest and weakest run was only 0.44 percentage points, indicating a high degree of reproducibility and robustness with respect to random initialization.

Although the epochs at which the best validation accuracies were obtained varied considerably across runs, ranging from epoch 224 to epoch 299, all runs converged to nearly identical performance levels. This suggests that different optimization trajectories lead to similar solutions and that the replicated implementation exhibits stable convergence behavior.

The seed study therefore demonstrates that the remaining discrepancy between the replication results and the performance reported in the original publication cannot be attributed primarily to random initialization. Instead, the consistency across seeds indicates that the observed accuracy level is a systematic property of the replicated implementation and experimental setup. Potential explanations for the remaining performance gap include differences between I-RAVEN and Balanced-RAVEN, implementation differences introduced during modernization of the codebase, or other experimental details not fully documented in the original work.

Overall, the five-seed evaluation confirms that the replicated preprocessed joint-training configuration consistently achieves approximately 82% validation accuracy and provides a reliable estimate of the expected performance of the reproduced model.
