The left_right experiment was conducted using the same technical setup as all previous experiments in order to preserve comparability across configurations. Training was performed for 300 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.005 and a weight decay of 0.01. The batch size for training was set to 128, while evaluation used a batch size of 32. The experiment relied on the generated I-RAVEN dataset together with the previously prepared train, validation, and test index files. Training logs, checkpoints, metadata, and summaries were continuously stored in the dedicated experiment directory throughout execution.

In contrast to the distribute_four and distribute_nine configurations, the left_right experiment achieved extremely high performance. After 300 epochs, the model reached a final training accuracy of approximately 99.98% and a final test accuracy of approximately 99.90%. Throughout the later training stages, both training and test accuracy remained consistently near-perfect, with only very small fluctuations between epochs.

The experiment shows almost no evidence of problematic overfitting. Although the training accuracy approached 100%, the test accuracy remained nearly identical, indicating that the learned representations generalized very effectively to unseen samples. Temporary fluctuations in individual epochs, such as the slight performance drop around epoch 295, recovered immediately in subsequent epochs and therefore do not indicate systematic instability or degradation. Instead, the overall convergence behavior remained highly stable across the entire training process.

These results are particularly important when compared to the previous distribute_four and distribute_nine experiments. While the dense grid-based configurations produced substantially lower accuracies around 45–47%, the left_right configuration achieved near-perfect performance comparable to the earlier center_single experiment. This suggests that the current replication does not suffer from a general failure of symbolic reasoning. Rather, the reproduced implementation appears specifically capable of solving relational tasks involving simpler spatial partitions while struggling primarily with highly distributed multi-object grid reasoning.

From a replication perspective, the left_right experiment strongly supports the validity of the reconstructed training pipeline. The achieved performance closely matches the near-perfect accuracies reported in the original paper for simpler spatial reasoning configurations, indicating that the adapted implementation successfully reproduces the intended model behavior in several important settings. At the same time, the sharp contrast between the successful center_single and left_right results and the weaker distribute_four and distribute_nine performance further strengthens the hypothesis that remaining discrepancies are concentrated specifically in the handling of complex distributed object interactions rather than in the overall training or optimization framework itself.
