The distribute_nine configuration used the corresponding symbolic feature dimensionality of 63 features.

The preprocessed distribute_nine experiment was performed to further evaluate whether the repository's preprocessing pipeline influences performance on the most challenging I-RAVEN configurations. Similar to the preprocessed distribute_four experiment, the model was trained using the serialized .pkl dataset generated by the preprocessing script rather than directly accessing the original dataset files through index-based loading.

The experiment completed successfully and produced a final test accuracy of approximately 45.4%. This result is remarkably close to the accuracy obtained in the corresponding raw-data experiment, which achieved approximately 46.8%. In contrast to the slight improvement observed for distribute_four, the preprocessed distribute_nine experiment produced a marginally lower result. The difference, however, is small and unlikely to be statistically meaningful without additional repeated runs using different random seeds.

The overall training dynamics closely resembled those observed in the raw-data experiment. Training accuracy stabilized at approximately 67%, while test accuracy remained near 45% throughout the final stages of training. The gap between training and test performance indicates that the model is capable of fitting a substantial portion of the training data but struggles to generalize effectively on this configuration. This behavior is consistent with the observations made during the original replication experiments and confirms that distribute_nine remains one of the most difficult configurations for the replicated implementation.

As in the previous preprocessing experiment, training became significantly faster because the symbolic representations were loaded directly from the preprocessed files. The reduction in preprocessing overhead improved execution efficiency but did not lead to a measurable increase in accuracy. Consequently, the results provide further evidence that the preprocessing stage itself is not responsible for the performance gap between the replication and the results reported by the original authors.

Taken together, the preprocessed distribute_four and distribute_nine experiments indicate that the preprocessing pipeline functions correctly and offers computational advantages, but it does not substantially affect model accuracy on the difficult distributed reasoning configurations. These findings suggest that the remaining discrepancies are more likely attributable to factors such as differences between I-RAVEN and Balanced-RAVEN, architectural implementation details, or compatibility-related modifications introduced during the replication process.
