The distribute_nine configuration used the corresponding symbolic feature dimensionality of 63 features.

The distribute_nine experiment was conducted using the same training procedure as the previous configurations in order to maintain comparability across the replication study. Training was performed for 300 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.005 and a weight decay of 0.01. The training batch size was set to 128 and the evaluation batch size to 32. The experiment employed the raw index-based loading pipeline, where symbolic representations were generated directly from the original I-RAVEN dataset during training and evaluation.

The experiment completed successfully and achieved a best validation accuracy of 47.91% at epoch 66. Performance improved gradually during the early stages of optimization and continued to increase throughout the first several dozen epochs before reaching its maximum value. After the peak was achieved, validation accuracy fluctuated moderately but remained close to the best value for the remainder of training. Training accuracy continued to increase and eventually exceeded 73%, whereas validation accuracy remained below 50%, indicating a substantial gap between training and generalization performance.

The results suggest that the distribute_nine configuration represents one of the most challenging reasoning tasks for the replicated SCL model. Compared to center_single and the directional configurations, the model was considerably less successful at generalizing to unseen examples. This outcome is consistent with the increased complexity of the task. The distribute_nine configuration contains a larger number of objects arranged in a 3×3 structure, resulting in a substantially larger combinatorial space of symbolic relationships that must be captured by the model.

Despite the relatively low predictive performance, the experiment remained technically stable throughout all 300 training epochs. Optimization proceeded normally, losses remained bounded, and no numerical instability or implementation failures were observed. The model consistently learned meaningful patterns from the training data, as evidenced by the continuous improvement in training accuracy, but was unable to transfer this performance fully to the validation set.

The similarity between the results obtained for distribute_nine and distribute_four suggests that configurations involving distributed multi-object reasoning are particularly difficult for the reproduced SCL implementation. Both tasks exhibit a substantial gap between training and validation accuracy and achieve validation performance well below the levels reported for simpler configurations. Consequently, these results provide further evidence that the largest discrepancies between the replication and the original SCL publication emerge primarily in the more complex spatial reasoning tasks.
